## Automation never felt small

When people talk about automation, they often describe it as a way to save time. That is true, but it always felt too small to me. Saving time is just the first visible effect. The more interesting part is that automation changes what kind of work remains for humans, and over the years I found that my own definition of automation kept expanding.

Early in my career, automation meant finding a repetitive task and making it disappear. Later, it meant creating systems that made repetition less likely in the first place. Now, with AI, I think we are reaching another stage where automation can help us build more automation. That is why the current moment feels important to me. It is not because I suddenly believe machines can do everything. It is because the direction of automation is becoming more explicit.

Looking back, each stage taught me a slightly different lesson about leverage. At first I learned that a small script can save hours. Then I learned that a workflow, an environment, or an interface contract can save teams from entire categories of recurring problems. Today I think the next layer is emerging: systems that do not only execute automated steps, but assist in constructing the automated systems themselves.

## When automation meant removing repetition

My earliest instinct around automation was simple. If I had to do something more than a few times, I started wondering why a computer was not doing it instead. At that stage the targets were obvious: repetitive file operations, tedious setup steps, ad hoc data conversion, deployment chores, and all the little pieces of engineering work that were too small to justify a big system but too annoying to keep doing by hand.

That phase is where many engineers first fall in love with automation. A short script, a helper tool, or a small pipeline can create leverage far beyond its size. You write a few lines once, and a task that used to demand attention becomes a background operation. The return on thought is unusually high. It teaches a very direct lesson: computers are not valuable only because they compute quickly. They are valuable because they absorb repetition without getting bored, distracted, or inconsistent.

At that point I still thought of automation mostly as a productivity tool. It was about removing friction from my own work. That was already useful, but it was still a narrow view. I was focused on the tasks that were visible in front of me, not yet on the larger structures that kept generating those tasks.

## When automation became systems

Over time my perspective changed. The real problem was not that one task was repetitive. The real problem was that the surrounding system kept producing manual work. Once you start seeing that, the target of automation gets bigger. You stop asking how to speed up a step and start asking why the process needs so many fragile steps at all.

This is where automation becomes less personal and more architectural. Instead of writing a script just to help myself, I started caring more about repeatable environments, reliable deployment paths, cleaner integration boundaries, and workflows that made the correct path the default path. The goal was no longer just efficiency. It was to make entire classes of mistakes or inconsistencies less likely.

That shift mattered because it changed what I thought engineers were really building. We are not only building features. We are also building the conditions under which future work happens. A good automated workflow does not merely save time. It changes team behavior. It reduces coordination cost. It makes quality easier to preserve. It lets people focus on exceptions and decisions rather than procedure.

Once I saw automation that way, it became hard to think of it as a side concern. Automation was no longer an accessory to engineering. It was part of the engineering itself. In many cases, the better system was the one that required less heroics from the humans operating it.

## When automation started helping build itself

This is why AI feels less like a sudden revolution and more like the next logical turn. In the earlier phases, automation executed the steps we already knew how to define. Now we have tools that can assist with defining and constructing those steps as well. They can draft code, suggest structure, generate transformations, scaffold repetitive logic, and help bridge the gap between an idea and a first working implementation.

That matters because it changes where engineering effort is spent. Traditionally, building automation still required a lot of manual construction. Even if the final workflow was highly automated, someone still had to handcraft much of the glue, the interface code, the configuration, the translation layer, and the repetitive implementation details. AI does not remove the need for engineering, but it can reduce the amount of manual effort required to produce the next layer of automation.

That is the idea I keep coming back to: the ultimate goal of automation is to automate itself. Not in the sense that humans disappear, but in the sense that the process of creating reliable automated behavior becomes increasingly assisted by other systems. We are moving from automating tasks, to automating workflows, to automating parts of the process that creates the workflows themselves.

To me, that is the most important way to understand the current wave of AI. The headline is not that machines can now perform impressive isolated tasks. The deeper story is that the distance between intent and implementation is shrinking. If an engineer can describe a transformation, a policy, an interface, or a repetitive pattern, there are now tools that can help turn that description into working artifacts much faster than before. That is a profound shift because the bottleneck starts moving away from raw implementation labor and toward problem definition, architecture, and verification.

## What still belongs to engineers

None of this makes engineers less important. If anything, it makes our responsibilities more visible. Goals still need to be chosen. Constraints still need to be set. Outputs still need to be verified. Trade-offs still need judgment. The better the automation becomes, the more important it is to be clear about what should be optimized, what must be protected, and what quality actually means.

This is one reason I am optimistic rather than anxious. I do not see AI as the end of engineering. I see it as another expansion of engineering leverage. When lower-level construction becomes easier, the center of gravity moves upward. Engineers spend less time spelling out every piece manually and more time deciding what is worth building, how it should behave, and how to tell whether it is correct. In that sense, better automation does not reduce responsibility. It concentrates it.

That does not reduce standards. It raises them. When generating an initial solution becomes cheaper, the differentiator is no longer just the ability to produce something. It is the ability to define the right problem, set the right boundaries, and recognize whether the result is robust, maintainable, and aligned with reality.

## Closing

So when I think about the progression of AI, I do not mainly think about replacement. I think about trajectory. For a long time we automated pieces of work. Then we automated workflows and environments around the work. Now we are starting to automate parts of the process that create those automated systems in the first place.

That feels like a natural continuation of the path automation has always been on. Each stage removes more manual construction and gives humans a clearer role in shaping intent. If that trend continues, the most valuable engineers will not be the ones who cling to every low-level repetitive step. They will be the ones who can define systems clearly, verify them rigorously, and direct automation toward outcomes that actually matter.

That is why I find this moment exciting. Automation is no longer only about teaching machines to do our work. It is increasingly about building machines that help us create the next machine. If that is where the field is heading, then the role of the engineer is not shrinking. It is becoming more strategic. And to me, that has always looked like the real destination.
